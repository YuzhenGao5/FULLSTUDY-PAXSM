### 2.01 Principal questions, hypotheses, and objectives

**Replace the complete answer with:**

This research aims to test a new way of answering questionnaires in an Extended Reality (XR) environment and investigate whether recording how people interact with the system can reveal useful information about their mental workload and attention during responding. We call this system the Process-Awareness XR Questionnaire System (PAXSM). It allows users to answer questions through a visual knob-based interaction and records the interaction trace.

The amended project addresses four related questions. First, can the knob-based interaction measure workload and provide acceptable usability compared with a traditional XR panel? Second, which interaction signals, such as response time, initiation latency, re-selections, pauses, reversals, and movement speed, differ between Careful and Speeded/Constrained responding? Third, can task parameters and observable task performance provide useful context for interpreting NASA-TLX Mental, Temporal, and Physical Demand ratings? Fourth, do structured, de-identified records that combine the final rating with calibrated task-side and response-process evidence help XR/HCI researchers review questionnaire responses?

The original mental-arithmetic and questionnaire studies are retained. A new colour-and-shape target-selection task provides Baseline, Mental, Temporal, Physical, and Combined profiles. The Combined data are held out and are not used to calibrate the interpretation rules. A formative study identifies researcher requirements before the participant studies, and a later researcher study evaluates the review records. The system does not automatically declare a response valid, invalid, or careless; the final decision remains with the researcher.

### 2.02 Background and justification

**Replace the complete answer with:**

Answering questionnaires with a knob is an embodied measurement approach: participants answer through a simple body action rather than pointing and clicking flat buttons. This may reduce the abstraction of questionnaire interaction and provide a richer record of how an answer was formed. Most XR questionnaires still reuse 2D click or slider interactions, and prior work has mainly focused on visual presentation rather than whether the answering modality can preserve immersion and expose useful process information.

A persistent challenge in questionnaire research is that a final rating alone provides limited information about the response process or the task episode that produced it. Existing careless-response detectors are generally designed for long scales and are difficult to apply to short in-situ XR questionnaires. In addition, a high workload rating may be difficult to interpret without information about the task parameters, performance, or data quality.

We address these gaps through four linked stages. A formative study identifies what evidence XR/HCI researchers need when reviewing subjective records. The retained PAXSM studies examine feasibility, usability, and Careful/Speeded response traces. A target-selection study tests candidate task profiles linked to Mental, Temporal, and Physical Demand while retaining all six NASA-TLX ratings so that cross-loading can be examined. Finally, researchers review de-identified held-out records to assess whether structured evidence supports review decisions. This staged design evaluates evidence cautiously: candidate indicators may be retained, classified as multidimensional or context-only, or rejected if they do not show a stable and interpretable relationship.

### 2.03 Contribution to new knowledge

**Replace the complete answer with:**

1. Extending embodied measurement to XR subjective measurement. The study evaluates whether a knob-based questionnaire remains feasible and usable in an immersive environment and compares it with a traditional panel-based implementation.

2. Item-level response-process evidence for short scales. PAXSM records response time, initiation latency, adjustments, pauses, reversals, movement speed, and other interaction signals. The study examines how these signals differ under Careful and Speeded/Constrained instructions and how they can be presented without automatically labelling a participant or response.

3. Task-linked context for subjective workload ratings. The target-selection study combines NASA-TLX responses with known task parameters and observable performance. This allows candidate indicators for Mental, Temporal, and Physical Demand to be assessed together with cross-loading and uncertainty.

4. Researcher-centred review records. The project develops and evaluates a structured record that binds a questionnaire response to its task episode, response process, provenance, missing-data information, and uncertainty. A formative study informs the design, and a blinded researcher review examines whether the record supports more informed and appropriately confident decisions.

Together, these contributions provide a cautious and reusable workflow for collecting and reviewing interpretable XR questionnaire records. The resulting software and reporting approach are intended to support future XR/HCI research without replacing researcher judgement.

### 2.04 Study design

**Replace the complete answer with:**

This amended study consists of four linked tasks and three participant groups, with a total of 48 unique participants. Twenty-four general adult participants complete Tasks 1 and 2. Twelve XR/HCI researchers complete Task 0, and a separate group of 12 XR/HCI researchers completes Task 3. Task 0 and Task 3 researchers will not overlap. All study activities take place at the Human Computer Interaction Lab (Room 303-521), Faculty of Science, University of Auckland, City Campus.

Task 0: Formative Researcher Study

Task 0 recruits 12 researchers who have experience conducting XR/HCI user studies or analysing subjective questionnaire data. In an approximately 45-minute session, they complete a short formative survey, describe their current questionnaire-data review practices, inspect synthetic examples of traditional questionnaire records and an early structured-evidence prototype, and provide written comments about useful evidence, provenance, uncertainty, and researcher control. No real participant records are shown. No audio or video is recorded; the researcher records de-identified written notes.

Task 1: Retained PAXSM Feasibility and Response-Process Study

The same 24 general participants later used in Task 2 first complete the two originally approved experiments in one approximately 75-minute session. The first experiment retains the 2 x 2 within-participant design: questionnaire system (traditional panel-based versus PAXSM) by mental-arithmetic difficulty (simple versus moderate). Each participant completes all four combinations. System and difficulty order are counterbalanced. After each arithmetic task, participants complete the full six-dimension NASA-TLX using the assigned questionnaire system. The SUS is administered once after participants first use each system.

The second retained experiment uses PAXSM to complete the UEQ-S under Careful and Speeded/Constrained instructions. Condition order is counterbalanced. These data provide independent response-process calibration and a normal Careful-response reference. The approved Polar H10, Meta Quest Pro eye tracking, Unity interaction logs, and brief written-note interview procedures are retained for Task 1.

Task 2: Dimension-Linked Target-Selection Study

The same 24 participants attend a second session of approximately 60 minutes. They use the Meta Quest Pro and controller to select objects defined by colour and shape. No Polar H10 or additional physiological data are collected in Task 2. A brief low-workload Baseline block is followed by three candidate profiles: Mental Demand (greater rule complexity, memory, or distractor requirements), Temporal Demand (shorter response windows or externally paced selections), and Physical Demand (greater reaching distance, movement, or target-selection effort). The three candidate profiles are counterbalanced; Baseline remains first for familiarisation and reference.

After each block, participants complete the full six-dimension NASA-TLX through PAXSM. After each NASA-TLX rating, they report their confidence on a five-point scale. The system records task parameters, accuracy, completion time, errors, timeouts, selected targets, corrections, and questionnaire interaction traces. All Baseline and candidate-profile questionnaire responses use a Careful instruction.

The final Combined block contains a mixture of the task characteristics and is identical for all participants. Participants are randomly allocated to one questionnaire-response condition: 12 Careful and 12 Speeded/Constrained. The instruction changes how the post-task questionnaire is answered, not the Combined task content. Combined data are held out: they are stored separately and are not used to select features, set thresholds, tune task-side indicators, or alter wording. They are accessed only after the response-process and task-side interpretation rules have been documented and frozen.

Task 3: Researcher Record-Review Study

A separate group of 12 XR/HCI researchers completes an approximately 45-60-minute blinded review study. Each reviewer views de-identified Combined records under two conditions: a Traditional Record containing the final NASA-TLX response and basic task outcomes, and a CARE-XR record containing the same information plus calibrated task-side and response-process evidence, provenance, missing-data information, and uncertainty. Record assignment and presentation order are counterbalanced, and the same reviewer does not see the same underlying record twice. Reviewers are not shown the Careful or Speeded/Constrained label. They indicate whether further review is needed, the likely source of concern, an appropriate action, their confidence, evidence used, and a short rationale. Review time is recorded.

### 9.02 Wider benefits

**Replace the complete answer with:**

The study may improve the interpretability of subjective self-report measures in XR. Traditional questionnaire records usually contain only a final rating. This project examines whether task parameters, task performance, response-process traces, provenance, missing-data information, and uncertainty can provide useful context without automatically labelling a participant or replacing researcher judgement.

The target-selection study can show whether candidate indicators relate to Mental, Temporal, and Physical Demand or instead cross-load across dimensions. The researcher-review study can provide preliminary evidence about whether structured records support more appropriate review decisions, evidence use, and confidence. ### 9.08 Risk provisions

**Replace the complete answer with:**

Some general participants may experience temporary dizziness, nausea, visual discomfort, headache, or disorientation associated with XR. Task 2 may also cause mild arm or shoulder fatigue from repeated target selection, and the Temporal or Speeded/Constrained conditions may cause mild time pressure, frustration, or tiredness. All XR tasks are completed while seated. The researcher remains present, offers short breaks between blocks, and provides additional breaks on request. Task parameters will be piloted for comfort, and the task will not require painful, forceful, or extreme movements.

Participants may remove the headset, decline a task, pause, or stop immediately without penalty. If dizziness or another adverse effect occurs, the procedure will be paused or terminated. The participant will remain seated and be given time and water until they feel stable. If symptoms persist, they will be advised not to drive or operate machinery and to seek medical advice if needed. The Speeded/Constrained instruction is explained as an experimental instruction rather than an assessment of ability.

Task 0 and Task 3 involve surveys and review of synthetic or de-identified research records and are expected to present minimal risk. Researcher participants are not asked to evaluate identifiable colleagues or make employment, academic, or clinical decisions. They may skip any open-text question or stop the session.

### General Participant Information Sheet - study summary

You are invited to take part in a two-session study evaluating questionnaire interaction and workload measurement in Extended Reality (XR). In Session 1, you will complete short mental-arithmetic tasks, answer NASA-TLX, SUS, and UEQ-S questionnaires using a traditional panel and a virtual-knob system, and take part in a brief interview. You receive a $10 supermarket voucher for each session you attend. Participation is voluntary. You may request a break, skip a question, stop a task, or withdraw without giving a reason. You may request removal of your data until two weeks after your final session.
